Finding 1: Average Daily Social Media Hours by Gender
Male teens recorded a slightly higher average daily social media usage (4.56 hours) compared to female teens (4.51 hours).
The difference is very small (0.05 hours), suggesting that social media usage patterns are relatively similar across genders in this dataset.

Finding 2: Social Media Platform Usage Distribution
The largest group of teens (391 users) reported using multiple social media platforms rather than relying on a single platform.
Among individual platforms, TikTok and Instagram were equally the most popular, each recording 240 users.
Facebook followed with 171 users, while YouTube had the lowest recorded usage at 158 users.
This suggests that multi-platform engagement is common among teens and that short-form and visual content platforms remain highly preferred.

Finding 3: Relationship Between Sleep Quality and Mental Health Risk
Teens with poor sleep quality recorded the highest average mental health risk score (16.84), followed by those with fair sleep quality (16.56).
Teens with good sleep quality had the lowest average mental health risk score (16.45).
Although the differences between categories are relatively small, the pattern suggests that lower sleep quality may be associated with slightly higher mental health risk among teens in this dataset.

Finding 4: Distribution of Digital Wellbeing Categories
Most teens (61.92%) fell under the Moderate digital wellbeing category, representing 743 users.
A smaller proportion of teens (25.50%) were classified as Healthy, accounting for 306 users.
The At Risk category recorded the lowest proportion at 12.58% (151 users).
These findings suggest that while the majority of teens demonstrated moderate digital wellbeing levels, a noticeable portion remained in the at-risk category, indicating opportunities for improving digital wellbeing practices.

Finding 5: Academic Performance Across Addiction Levels
Average academic performance remained relatively stable across all addiction levels, with values ranging between 2.90 and 3.06.
The highest average academic performance was recorded at addiction level 10 (3.06), while the lowest average performance appeared at addiction levels 2 and 5 (2.90).
Overall, there was no strong upward or downward trend in academic performance as addiction levels increased.
This suggests that within this dataset, addiction level alone may not show a strong relationship with academic performance, and additional factors may influence academic outcomes.
